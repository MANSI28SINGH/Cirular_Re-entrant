# Parametric Redesign of Circular Re-Entrant Unit Cell for Tunable Negative Poisson’s Ratio

This repository contains the CAD models, finite element analysis (FEA) setups, and parametric study data for the **CR-X** auxetic unit cell structure. The project focuses on introducing an internal X-shaped reinforcement into a conventional circular re-entrant (CR) lattice to optimize structural stability and tune its negative Poisson's ratio (NPR) behavior.

---

## Project Overview

* **Objective:** Design a modified auxetic lattice structure (CR-X) to achieve high structural robustness while maintaining highly tunable energy-absorbing properties.
* **Core Innovation:** Embedding an internal X-shaped geometry within a traditional circular re-entrant unit cell to minimize stress concentrations and govern lateral contraction under axial loading.
* **Key Finding:** Thinner ligament designs increase bending and rotational flexibility, yielding up to a 7% increase in the absolute Negative Poisson's Ratio (NPR) values.

---

## Methodology & Engineering Framework

### 1. Geometric Modeling
* **Software:** CATIA Platform
* **Configuration:** 2D periodic auxetic structures generated by arranging unit cells in a $3 \times 3$ array along both the X and Y axes.
* **Key Dimensions:** Cell Length ($L$) = 32.2 mm, Height ($H$) = 27.95 mm, Radius ($R$) = 14.59 mm.

### 2. Material Characterization
* **Base Material:** Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS) Plastic
* **Source:** Validated material data sourced from the **ANSYS Granta Material Database**, ensuring standardized, uniform, and reproducible simulation parameters (Young's Modulus, Yield Strength).

### 3. Finite Element Analysis (FEA)
* **Environment:** ANSYS Workbench (Explicit Dynamic Solver)
* **Element Type:** Tetrahedral solid elements with a uniform element size of 1.2 mm.
* **Boundary Conditions:** Lower boundary fully fixed ($U_x = U_y = U_z = 0$); upper boundary subjected to a displacement-controlled compressive load ($-U_y$).

---

## Technical Formulas & Math

The true auxetic response (Poisson's ratio, $\nu_{xy}$) was computed during the linear elastic phase of deformation by extracting the true axial strain ($\varepsilon_y$) and transverse strain ($\varepsilon_x$):

$$\nu_{xy} = -\frac{\varepsilon_x}{\varepsilon_y}$$

Where:
* $\varepsilon_y$ is the strain along the primary compressive loading direction.
* $\varepsilon_x$ is the measured transverse expansion/contraction perpendicular to loading.

## Key Results & Discussion

### 1. The Auxetic Performance Trade-Off
Both the conventional CR and redesigned CR-X models consistently exhibited negative Poisson's ratio behavior across all applied strain levels (up to $\varepsilon = 0.25$). 

| Applied Strain ($\varepsilon$) | $\nu_{xy}$ (Modified CR-X) | $\nu_{xy}$ (Conventional CR) |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| 0.05 | -1.25 | -1.65 |
| 0.10 | -1.10 | -1.53 |
| 0.20 | -1.00 | -1.40 |
| 0.25 | -0.94 | -1.21 |

> **Insight:** The conventional CR cell displays higher absolute NPR values. However, the internal X-shape in the **CR-X design acts as a mechanical reinforcement**, heavily increasing structural stiffness and stability at a marginal cost to the absolute NPR, making it perfect for rugged structural energy absorbers.

### 2. Parametric Tuning via Ligament Thickness ($t$)
A systematic study varying the internal ligament thickness ($t$) from $0.65\text{ mm}$ to $1.85\text{ mm}$ revealed that auxetic behavior can be directly tuned through geometric modifications alone:

* **Thinner Ligaments ($t = 0.65\text{ mm}$):** Achieved an NPR of **-1.008** due to lowered bending resistance and higher rotational freedom at the joints.
* **Thicker Ligaments ($t = 1.85\text{ mm}$):** Restricted joint rotation due to enhanced bending stiffness, shifting the NPR to **-0.95**.

---

## Conclusions & Applications
The **CR-X design** establishes a highly reliable path for tuning the mechanics of next-generation metamaterials. By altering only the ligament geometry, we achieve predictable energy-absorbing characteristics and superior structural stability. This makes it an exemplary candidate for protective sports equipment, aerospace components, and automotive crashworth systems.
